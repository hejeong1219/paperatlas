# EGFR-Mutant NSCLC and Immunotherapy Mismatch

EGFR-mutant NSCLC sits in a chronic mismatch state for immunotherapy: it is highly targetable with TKIs, yet often poorly responsive to checkpoint blockade even after TKI failure. The problem is not captured by one variable alone, but by lineage plasticity, low baseline immunogenicity, pathway-driven immune rewiring, and weak biomarker transferability from smoking-associated NSCLC.

## Key Points

- EGFR inhibitor resistance can emerge through canonical secondary alterations, histologic transformation, EMT, and dynamic loss or gain of resistance lesions over time.
- EGFR-mutant disease often carries lower TMB overall, but the higher-TMB subset within EGFR-mutant cancers may still behave worse on TKIs rather than more immunogenic.
- Prospective checkpoint studies after TKI failure show very limited efficacy even when PD-L1 or TMB appears non-zero.
- Bypass events such as MET amplification can directly inhibit innate immune sensing pathways such as STING, providing a mechanistic bridge from targeted resistance to checkpoint failure.
- This axis is best understood as a genotype-linked immune mismatch rather than a simple low-PD-L1 problem.
