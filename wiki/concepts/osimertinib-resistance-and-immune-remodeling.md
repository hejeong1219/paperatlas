# Osimertinib Resistance and Immune Remodeling

First-line osimertinib resistance is only partially genomic. A substantial fraction of progression appears to involve non-genetic state changes and remodeling of the tumor-immune ecosystem.

## Key Points

- Paired ctDNA from FLAURA identifies MET amplification and EGFR C797S as recurrent mechanisms, but most progressing patients have no detectable genomic explanation in plasma.
- Microenvironment-focused work shows that resistant disease can shift toward lower T-cell infiltration and more M2-like macrophage states.
- Resistant-cell exosomes seem to participate in that rewiring, implying active crosstalk rather than incidental immune depletion.
- Earlier EGFR-TKI resistance studies had already shown that EMT, histologic transformation, and bypass signaling are part of the same evolutionary landscape.
- MET-driven resistance is especially important because it can also weaken STING-linked innate immune sensing, further worsening checkpoint susceptibility.
- This helps explain why EGFR-mutant lung cancer remains difficult territory for checkpoint immunotherapy even after targeted-therapy failure.
- The combined lesson is that post-osimertinib decisions should integrate genotype and immune state, not assume that resistance is exhausted by mutation calling.
