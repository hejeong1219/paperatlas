# Breast cancer quantitative proteome and proteogenomic landscape

Large-scale breast cancer proteogenomic study showing that quantitative proteomics refines subtype structure, reveals protein modules, and exposes limits of RNA-only classification.

## Key Points

- Quantifies thousands of proteins across breast tumors and shows that the global proteome can recapitulate established breast cancer subtype structure.
- Further subdivides poor-prognosis basal-like and luminal B tumors by immune-component infiltration, suggesting standard classifications remain incomplete.
- Highlights that copy-number effects are dampened at the protein layer and that mRNA-based prognostic panels work partly because they contain genes with unusually high mRNA-protein concordance.
- Uses proteome-based networks to identify functional modules and improve interpretation of the poorly defined normal-like subtype.
- Notes detection of protein products mapping to non-coding genomic regions, raising the possibility of tumor-specific immunotherapeutic targets beyond canonical coding genes.

## Relevance

- Relevant to the ptmanchor manuscript because it reinforces the broader argument that direct protein-level measurement can refine classification and interpretation beyond RNA alone.
- Useful as supporting background for abundance-aware signaling and subtype interpretation in clinical tumor cohorts.
- More proteome-focused than phosphoproteome-focused, but still a strong fit for the expanding PTM-side background corpus.
