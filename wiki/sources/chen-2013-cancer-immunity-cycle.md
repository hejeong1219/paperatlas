# Oncology Meets Immunology: The Cancer-Immunity Cycle

This review provides the canonical stepwise model for how productive antitumor immunity is generated, amplified, and then often interrupted inside tumors.

## Key Points

- Frames antitumor immunity as a cycle rather than a single event: antigen release, antigen presentation, T-cell priming, trafficking, infiltration, recognition, and killing.
- Argues that successful immunotherapy often depends on identifying which step of the cycle is broken in a given tumor rather than assuming a uniform defect.
- Places PD-L1-mediated inhibition inside a broader systems view in which checkpoint blockade removes only one brake on the cycle.
- Emphasizes that suppressive tumor microenvironmental factors can explain why earlier immune therapies underperformed and why rational combinations are needed.
- Functions as the conceptual predecessor to later immune phenotype and biomarker frameworks, including the cancer-immune set point.

## Relevance

- This is a foundational framework paper for almost every later discussion of immune exclusion, checkpoint resistance, and combination immunotherapy.
- It is especially useful as a bridge between tumor-intrinsic resistance mechanisms and microenvironmental failure states.
