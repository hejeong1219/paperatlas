# Proteogenomics connects somatic mutations to signalling in breast cancer

Landmark CPTAC breast cancer study showing how integrated proteome and phosphoproteome analysis connects somatic alterations to pathway activation and therapeutic target nomination.

## Key Points

- Performs quantitative proteomic and phosphoproteomic analysis on genomically annotated human breast cancers from TCGA.
- Shows that proteogenomic analysis can clarify consequences of chromosomal loss and amplification in ways not fully visible at the mRNA layer.
- Identifies stromal-enriched protein groups and phosphoproteome-defined pathway clusters, including signaling features not readily resolved from transcriptomics alone.
- Highlights highly phosphorylated kinases beyond ERBB2, including CDK12, PAK1, PTK2, RIPK2, and TLK2, as candidate therapeutic targets.
- Establishes a template for connecting somatic mutation and copy-number context to real signaling output in tumor tissue.

## Relevance

- Extremely relevant to the ptmanchor manuscript because it is one of the clearest high-impact examples of why protein and phosphoprotein interpretation matters for pathway and kinase inference.
- Serves as a foundational reference for linking genomic lesions to downstream phosphosite and kinase-state consequences in human cancer.
- Strong landmark background for any PTM-aware framework that claims protein-level and phospho-level readouts can reveal biology missed by RNA alone.
