# Nous-209 neoantigen vaccine for cancer prevention in Lynch syndrome (NCT05078866)

_Nature Medicine 32:1002–1011, 2026 — phase 1b/2 trial._ PMID: [41545594](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41545594/) · DOI: [10.1038/s41591-025-04182-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-04182-9) · ClinicalTrials.gov: [NCT05078866](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05078866)

## Summary

This is a paradigm-shift trial: an off-the-shelf neoantigen vaccine for **cancer interception** in healthy Lynch-syndrome carriers, not therapy in established cancer. The intervention, Nous-209, is a heterologous prime-boost (GAd-209-FSP great-ape adenovirus prime + MVA-209-FSP modified vaccinia Ankara boost) encoding 209 frameshift peptides (FSPs) that recur across microsatellite-unstable (MSI) tumors. In cohort 1 (n = 45 cancer-free LS carriers, MSH2 47% / MSH6 24% / MLH1 18% / PMS2 11%), prime+boost induced neoantigen-specific T-cell responses in 100% of 37 evaluable participants, with mean peak ELISpot ≈ 1,100 IFN-γ SFC per 10⁶ PBMC, durable in 85% at 1 year, recognizing multiple FSPs and including both CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T-cell components. Cytotoxic activity was confirmed in vitro for >100 immunogenic FSPs, and these FSPs are present in independent LS-MSI colorectal precancer/cancer datasets — i.e., the vaccine targets antigens that LS carriers actually generate as they progress. Safety was acceptable for a prevention setting (no related SAE; injection-site + fatigue dominate, ≤4% grade-3). The trial supports development of FSP vaccines for **immune interception** before cancer arises.

## Key Points

- **Indication is prevention, not treatment**: enrolls healthy LS carriers with no active cancer in 6 months pre-screening — this is a fundamentally different risk-benefit calculus than therapeutic neoantigen vaccines.
- **Off-the-shelf shared neoantigens**: 209 FSPs derived from coding-region indels in microsatellite repeats — these are **recurrent across LS-MSI tumors**, so one product fits all carriers (vs personalized vaccines that need patient-specific design).
- **Heterologous prime-boost**: GAd20 prime → MVA boost — vector switching avoids anti-vector immunity from killing the boost; chosen because GAd and MVA each support large multi-antigen inserts (209 FSPs).
- **Primary immunogenicity endpoint**: 100% (37/37 evaluable) developed neoantigen-specific T-cell responses; mean peak ~1,100 IFN-γ SFC per 10⁶ PBMC; 85% durable at 1 year — these are very high response rates for a vaccine targeting non-HLA-prepared neoantigens.
- **Both CD4 and CD8 induced** with breadth across multiple FSPs (paper specifies >100 immunogenic FSPs with demonstrated in-vitro cytotoxicity), supporting broad immune coverage rather than single-epitope dominance.
- **External validation**: the same FSPs found immunogenic in vaccinees were also detected in independent LS-MSI CRC precancer/cancer datasets, closing the loop that the vaccine targets antigens the body will actually encounter during oncogenesis.
- **Safety acceptable for prevention**: no treatment-related SAE; injection-site reactions 91%/76% (prime/boost), fatigue 80%/53%, only 4% grade 3 — compatible with use in a healthy at-risk population.
- **Cohort 2** (re-vaccination at 1 year) is reserved for a follow-up paper, important for understanding annual booster strategy.

## Methods

Phase 1b/2, single-arm, open-label, multicenter cancer-interception trial. 45 LS carriers age ≥18 with confirmed pathogenic MMR mutation, no active cancer ≥6 months. Vaccination: GAd20-209-FSPs IM at week 0, MVA-209-FSPs IM at week 8. Primary endpoints: safety (AE rate) and immunogenicity (IFN-γ ELISpot pre/post). Secondary: changes in colorectal adenoma/advanced neoplasia/carcinoma at follow-up colonoscopy. CD4/CD8 attribution by intracellular cytokine staining; FSP cytotoxicity in vitro using HLA-matched effector cells against FSP-pulsed targets. External FSP validation against independent LS CRC datasets.

## Limitations

- **Single-arm and short follow-up for the primary outcome of interest (cancer reduction)**: phase 1b/2 demonstrates immunogenicity, not reduced cancer incidence — that requires a randomized prevention trial with multi-year follow-up.
- **Selection bias**: cancer-free participants by design; the trial does not address LS carriers with active surveillance findings (high-grade dysplasia or carcinoma).
- **Adenoma reduction is secondary**: the trial powered for safety/immunogenicity, not for adenoma counts; effect on the actual prevention endpoint is hypothesis-generating.
- **Predominantly white population (91%)**: HLA distribution may not generalize to East Asian populations (different HLA-A/B prevalence), affecting which FSPs are presentable.
- **No B-cell readout**: FSPs may be highly immunogenic for humoral responses too, but the paper focuses on T-cell ELISpot — antibody responses to FSPs in LS carriers are not characterized here.
- **MMR gene heterogeneity**: enrollment is unbalanced (MSH2 47%, PMS2 only 11%); whether immunogenicity is uniform across MMR-gene subtypes is not statistically resolved.

## Open Questions

- Will adenoma reduction become detectable in the longer follow-up cohort, and what's the minimal effect size that would justify expanding this to broader prevention populations?
- Does annual revaccination (cohort 2) sustain or boost immunogenicity, and is the 1-year durability (85%) sufficient or do carriers need a different schedule?
- Do vaccine-induced T cells actually infiltrate LS adenomas before they progress? The paper does not show TIL data; this gap matters for mechanism.
- How will this generalize to non-LS sporadic MSI-H cancers (CRC, endometrial, gastric) where FSP repertoire overlaps but tumor biology differs?
- Should Nous-209 be combined with anti-PD-1 in early high-grade dysplasia (the borderline between prevention and treatment), and does that retain the safety profile?
