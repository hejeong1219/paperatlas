# Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition Is Associated with a Distinct Tumor Microenvironment Including Elevation of Inflammatory Signals and Multiple Immune Checkpoints in Lung Adenocarcinoma

This paper is useful because it complicates the simple hot-versus-cold view of tumor immunity: mesenchymal lung adenocarcinomas can look inflamed yet still be deeply suppressive, with multiple checkpoint axes elevated at once.

## Key Points

- Lou et al. integrated transcriptomic, protein, and immunohistochemical analyses across multiple lung adenocarcinoma cohorts and linked EMT status to a distinct inflammatory but immunosuppressed microenvironment.
- Mesenchymal tumors showed higher expression not only of `PD-L1`, `PD-L2`, and `PD-1`, but also `TIM-3`, `B7-H3`, `BTLA`, and `CTLA-4`, alongside regulatory T-cell enrichment.
- The study matters because it argues that inflammation alone is not enough to infer immune susceptibility; an EMT state can coexist with immune activation signals and compensatory inhibitory circuitry.
- In the resistance framing of this wiki, EMT acts as a state-based interface between targeted-therapy escape, stromal remodeling, and checkpoint biology.
- The paper also helps explain why some lung cancers can appear biologically inflamed yet remain clinically difficult to convert with a single immune-checkpoint axis.
