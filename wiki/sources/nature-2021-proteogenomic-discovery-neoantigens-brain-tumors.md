# Proteogenomic discovery of neoantigens facilitates personalized multi-antigen targeted T cell immunotherapy for brain tumors

Low-input human brain tumor study showing that proteogenomics can recover multiple immunogenic tumor-specific peptides, with aberrant splice junctions as a dominant antigen source.

## Key Points

- Develops a low-input proteogenomic workflow that combines tumor DNA and RNA sequencing with mass spectrometry to identify tumor-specific peptides from limited tissue.
- Focuses on pediatric brain tumors, especially medulloblastoma, where low mutational burden makes mutation-only neoantigen discovery insufficient.
- Finds that aberrant splice junctions are a major source of neoantigens, expanding the field beyond simple SNV-centered search strategies.
- Shows that identified peptides can generate polyclonal and polyfunctional T-cell responses and support multi-antigen personalized cell-therapy manufacturing.
- Frames tumor-specific peptide selection as a way to reduce central tolerance and limit off-tumor toxicity compared with broader tumor-associated antigen strategies.

## Relevance

- Useful for the B-cell neoantigen proposal as a human proof-of-principle that proteogenomics can recover actionable neoantigen space even when canonical mutation burden is low.
- Strengthens the argument that splice-derived and other noncanonical sources should stay inside the candidate-generation layer of future neoantigen pipelines.
- More T-cell-centered than B-cell-centered, but still valuable as a translational template for personalized antigen discovery and validation.
