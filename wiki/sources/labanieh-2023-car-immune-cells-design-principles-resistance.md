# CAR immune cells: design principles, resistance and the next generation

This perspective is a high-value synthesis of why current CAR therapies succeed in liquid cancers, why they fail, and which engineering choices are most promising for the next generation.

## Key Points

- Distinguishes two major resistance classes: target-side escape such as antigen modulation or lineage change, and cell-side failure such as inadequate potency, persistence, or exhaustion.
- Treats `antigen density threshold` as a design parameter, arguing that receptor architecture can be tuned either to capture low-antigen escape states or to improve safety in normal tissues.
- Reviews multiple target-escape routes including mutation, deletion, splicing, lineage switching, trogocytosis, glycosylation, and antigen shedding.
- Summarizes how costimulatory domains, tonic signaling, T-cell state, and tumor burden shape expansion, persistence, and exhaustion.
- Highlights multispecific, logic-gated, regulatable, and armored designs as concrete strategies to mitigate escape and improve solid-tumor performance.

## Relevance

- This is one of the best umbrella papers for integrating older CD19 escape studies with newer engineering responses.
- It is especially helpful for linking mechanism papers to actionable design principles rather than leaving them as isolated observations.
