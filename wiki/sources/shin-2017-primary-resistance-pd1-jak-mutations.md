# Primary Resistance to PD-1 Blockade Mediated by JAK1/2 Mutations

This paper extends the checkpoint-resistance story from acquired escape into primary nonresponse, arguing that high mutation burden alone is not enough when tumor cells are intrinsically unable to sense interferon.

## Key Points

- Shin et al. identified loss-of-function `JAK1` or `JAK2` mutations in rare nonresponding tumors treated with PD-1 blockade, including melanoma and mismatch-repair-deficient colorectal cancer.
- Despite high mutation burden, these tumors did not respond, making the paper an early warning against treating hypermutation as a sufficient biomarker.
- Functional studies showed that `JAK1/2`-mutant melanoma cell lines failed to induce PD-L1 or other interferon-response programs after interferon-gamma stimulation.
- The mechanistic claim is sharper than "immune coldness": the tumor can be antigenic on paper yet biologically insulated from a key effector cytokine pathway needed for checkpoint benefit.
- Together with Zaretsky 2016, this paper helps separate two timelines for the same axis: pre-existing interferon-pathway incompetence versus interferon-pathway loss selected during therapy.
