# MET Amplification Attenuates Lung Tumor Response to Immunotherapy by Inhibiting STING

This paper links a canonical targeted-therapy bypass event, `MET` amplification, to a direct innate-immunity defect, arguing that MET can suppress `STING` and thereby create an immune-insensitive lung cancer state.

## Key Points

- Zhang et al. found that patients with lung cancer harboring `MET` amplification had poorer responses to immune-checkpoint blockade and lower progression-free survival.
- MET-amplified tumors showed decreased `STING` expression and reduced antitumor T-cell infiltration, with single-cell analyses pointing to a more suppressive immune composition.
- Mechanistically, oncogenic MET signaling promoted phosphorylation of `UPF1`, altered STING 3'UTR regulation, and reduced tumor-cell STING expression.
- The paper is especially useful because it directly connects oncogenic bypass signaling to innate immune sensing rather than treating targeted-therapy resistance and immunotherapy resistance as separate worlds.
- It also provides a concrete combination rationale: MET inhibition may restore checkpoint sensitivity in MET-driven resistant lung cancers.
