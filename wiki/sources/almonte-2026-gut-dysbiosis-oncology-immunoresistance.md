# Gut dysbiosis in oncology: a risk factor for immunoresistance

This review reframes microbiome involvement in immunotherapy from a positive-response story to a resistance story, arguing that dysbiosis should be treated as a bona fide host-level risk factor for failed antitumor immunity.

## Key Points

- Almonte et al. synthesize evidence that disrupted gut microbial ecology can weaken antigen presentation, T-cell priming, myeloid regulation, and treatment responsiveness across oncology settings.
- The paper is useful because it moves beyond identifying "good responder taxa" and instead asks what types of microbial disruption repeatedly map onto immunoresistance.
- Dysbiosis is framed as a systems-level problem linked to antibiotics, diet, inflammation, barrier dysfunction, and cancer treatment itself, which makes it both clinically relevant and difficult to standardize.
- In the context of this wiki, the review strengthens the argument that resistance is not only tumor-intrinsic or stromal, but can also be set by host ecosystem failure upstream of the tumor.
- It complements earlier microbiota-response reviews by making the negative state, not just the desirable state, analytically central.
