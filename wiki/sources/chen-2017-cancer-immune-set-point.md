# Elements of cancer immunity and the cancer-immune set point

This review extends the cancer-immunity-cycle model into a multivariable framework for why superficially similar tumors can have very different immunotherapy outcomes.

## Key Points

- Defines the `cancer-immune set point` as the effective threshold that must be crossed for productive antitumor immunity to emerge.
- Argues that tumor genomics, antigenicity, checkpoint signaling, host state, microbiota, infection history, and environmental exposures all contribute to that threshold.
- Organizes tumors into immune-inflamed, immune-excluded, and immune-desert phenotypes and links those states to checkpoint sensitivity.
- Treats PD-L1 as a useful but incomplete biomarker, arguing for composite immune profiling rather than single-marker prediction.
- Connects neoantigen burden and T-cell recruitment to broader ecological constraints in the tumor microenvironment rather than treating mutation count alone as destiny.

## Relevance

- This is one of the most durable conceptual papers for interpreting primary resistance to checkpoint blockade and for thinking about biomarker combinations.
- It gives the wiki a stable language for integrating tumor-intrinsic and host-level explanations of immune success or failure.
